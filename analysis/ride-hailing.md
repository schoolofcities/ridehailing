# Ridehailing in Toronto: (snazzy title?)

The presence of ridehailing (e.g. Uber and Lyft) has grown substantially over the past 15 years in Toronto. The number of drivers, trips, kilometres driven, and impacts on congestion have grown steadily. Below we visualize this growth and ponder how a small tax on the $1.8 Billion dollars per year (2025) Torontonians pay for rideshailing services could fund more sustainable transportation options.


# Charting growth in ridehailing

To operate in the City of Toronto, private transportation companies are required to submit detailed data to the City (trip origins and destinations, fares, driver hours, vehicle details) under a [Data Sharing Agreement](https://www.toronto.ca/services-payments/permits-licences-bylaws/private-transportation-companies-uberfacedrive-drivers/private-transportation-companies/data-sharing-agreement/). Some of this data has since been published through Toronto's [Open Data Portal](https://open.toronto.ca/dataset/private-transportation-companies-summary-and-trip-data/).

The following charts, created from the City's recently released data, show relatively steady growth in the number of active ridehailing vehicles per day, trips per day, and the total kilometres driven per day.

Overall, more motor vehicles on Toronto streets means greater risks to pedestrians and cyclists, more congestion, and thus longer travel times for drivers and worsened air quality for everyone.

[Chart 2) Trips per day, daily and 30 day rolling average]

If ridehailing ceased to exist in Toronto, certainly some of these trips would be replaced by traditional taxis or personal vehicles, and the aforementioned negative impacts would not change much. However, the effect of ridehailing is amplified by deadheading, the ~30% of total kilometres driven empty (the red area on the chart above) add vehicles to the road without moving anyone, which is worse for congestion than a comparable private car trip. 

Some proportion of these trips could certainly be replaced by more sustainable modes, walking, cycling, and public transit. In a [previous analysis](https://osf.io/download/hvbma/) using 2016 survey data we found that more than 30% of Uber trips in Toronto had viable alternatives via public transit. 
Looking at the recently released data, for 2025, around 35 million trips (~41% of total) were less than 5km. Many of these shorter trips likely have viable alternatives via transit or cycling (e.g. it takes about 20 minutes to cycle 5km). 

[Chart 3) Simple distribution of trip distances (bars) in 2025]

(The City's recently released data does not include detailed geographic data on the origins and destinations of each trip to accurately assess comparable transit, cycling, or walking travel times and costs. Having this data would allow for more accurate assessment of what percentage of Uber/Lyft trips have viable sustainable alternatives).



# Taxing ridehailing to fund sustainable alternatives

Under Toronto's Vehicle-for-Hire Bylaw, Uber and Lyft are licensed as Private Transportation Companies (PTCs). The companies pay a $24,424.35 one-time application fee, then $18.32 per driver per year plus $0.35 per trip in licensing fees, with an additional $8.65 per driver and $0.10 per trip directed to an accessibility fund (which supports on-demand, wheelchair accessible vehicle-for-hire service).

[Chart 4) Total fares ($) per day, daily and 30 day rolling average]

In 2025, there were 87 Million trips and __ drivers. Multiplying by the fees noted above, is $30.8 Million paid to the City ($39.6 Million when the accessibility fund fees are included). These total fees, while sizable on their own, are still quite small compared to the $1.8 Billion in total fares that were paid in 2025, especially when considering the impact these vehicles have on congestion and public safety.

Adding just a $1.00 to each trip, or an extra 5%, would raise an additional $87,000,000 for the City (based on 2025 data).

The first scenario puts relatively greater tax on shorter trips, since shorter trips tend to have a smaller fare (e.g. $1 on a $10 trip within a neighbourhood relative to $1 on a $50 cross-city trip). Shorter trips are also more likely to have viable walking, cycling, or public transit alternatives.

Higher fees would raise costs for users. This could persuade some trips towards public transit or active modes, meaning a modest reduction in congestion, and increase in transit fare revenue.

There's also an equity angle: ride-hailing skews heavily toward higher-income riders, while transit is relied on most by lower-income residents. A levy would effectively be progressive.
